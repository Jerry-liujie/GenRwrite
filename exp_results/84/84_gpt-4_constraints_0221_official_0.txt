1. Use explicit JOIN syntax instead of comma-separated tables in the FROM clause: This rule can be applied when multiple tables are being joined in a query, especially when there are complex relationships between the tables.
2. Move conditions from the WHERE clause to the ON clause in relevant JOINs: This rule can be applied when there are conditions in the WHERE clause that are based on columns from two different tables that are being joined.
3. Remove unnecessary table joins: This rule can be applied when a table is included in the JOIN clause but its columns are not used in the SELECT, WHERE, or ORDER BY clauses.
